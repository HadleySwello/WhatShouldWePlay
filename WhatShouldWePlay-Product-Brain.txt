## Product Summary

**WhatShouldWePlay?** helps board game enthusiasts quickly decide what to play from a large game library, without burning game-night energy on 20 minutes of indecision.

It is built for hobbyists (often with BoardGameGeek-tracked collections) and groups that struggle with decision fatigue, social pressure, or analysis paralysis. Instead of endless back-and-forth, the app narrows the field based on the group’s constraints and preferences, then resolves the final choice in a way that feels playful, fair, and decisive.

By leveraging the collection data players already maintain in BoardGameGeek (and a planned in-app BGG library dataset for users without BGG accounts), it turns an overwhelming shelf into a manageable set of real options. The weighted selection flow allows multiple people to influence the outcome, so the final pick feels structured rather than arbitrary.

The goal is simple: reduce decision fatigue, preserve game-night momentum, and get to the table faster.

# Core Audience

## Audience Positioning

WhatShouldWePlay? is designed for people who experience cognitive load around choosing what to play — especially in group settings. This includes neurodivergent players who experience decision fatigue, anxiety around choosing “wrong,” or overwhelm when faced with too many options.

The app intentionally reduces ambiguity and externalizes the burden of choice.

## Primary Theme

People who experience friction around choosing what to play — especially in group settings — regardless of collection size.

Collection size increases overwhelm, but the real trigger is cognitive load + social pressure.

---

## Persona 1: The Neurodivergent Completionist with Anxiety (Primary)

**Profile**

- Loves systems
- May hyperfocus on optimization
- Can spiral when comparing too many options
- May feel responsible for “choosing correctly”

**Game Night Stress**

- Choosing wrong
- Letting someone down
- Analysis paralysis
- Overthinking edge cases

**Motivation**

- Clear narrowing steps
- Externalized decision-making
- Fairness and shared influence

**Why They Use the App**

- Offloads decision burden
- Weighted selection feels equitable
- Reduces emotional load of being the decider

---

## Persona 2: The Chaos Shelf Collector

**Profile**

- Owns 50–300+ games
- Buys faster than they play
- Forgets what’s in the collection
- Loves browsing their shelf but dreads narrowing it down

**Game Night Stress**

- Wasting time deciding
- Defaulting to the same 3 games
- Realizing a great game hasn’t been played in a year

**Motivation**

- Rotate games into play
- Rediscover hidden gems
- Feel like the collection is alive, not stagnant

**Why They Use the App**

- Quick filtering
- Surfaces forgotten games
- Makes selection feel playful instead of stressful

---

## Persona 3: The Casual but Overwhelmed Owner

**Profile**

- Owns 10–75 games
- Plays occasionally
- Doesn’t remember rules complexity offhand
- Wants game night to feel smooth, not chaotic

**Game Night Stress**

- Wasting 20 minutes debating
- Someone being disappointed with the choice
- Picking something too long or too heavy

**Motivation**

- Keep energy high
- Avoid awkward group tension
- Get to the fun part quickly

**Why They Use the App**

- Structured narrowing
- Shared input via weighted selection
- Removes social pressure from one person

# Emotional Positioning

## Core Emotional Outcome

When WhatShouldWePlay? works perfectly, the room feels:

- Relieved
- Energized
- Playful
- Ready to start playing

The group moves from **indecision → anticipation → action** quickly and smoothly.

Instead of debating or negotiating, the group performs a small ritual together and arrives at a decision everyone can accept.

The result feels fair, satisfying, and slightly magical.

---

## Emotional Friction It Removes

WhatShouldWePlay? reduces:

- Decision fatigue
- Social pressure around choosing the “right” game
- Endless comparison between options
- The awkward moment where no one wants to decide
- Anxiety about disappointing someone

The app removes the burden of personal responsibility for the choice.

Instead of **someone choosing**, the group **consults the ritual.**

---

## Emotional Archetype

### Core Archetype

**The Mystic Ritual-Keeper**

WhatShouldWePlay? acts like a small, benevolent oracle that appears when a group gets stuck deciding what to play.

It does not judge or rush the players. Instead, it guides them through a simple ritual that leads to a fair and satisfying outcome.

The app doesn’t *decide for the group* — it **consults the fates on their behalf.**

---

### Emotional Role in the Room

When the group stalls in indecision, the app becomes the neutral third party that resolves the moment.

Instead of someone having to take responsibility for choosing, the group performs a small ritual together:

1. Narrow the possibilities
2. Offer their preferences
3. Let fate decide

This transforms an awkward social negotiation into a **shared moment of anticipation and reveal**.

---

### Emotional Outcome

When the ritual completes, the room should feel:

- Relieved
- Energized
- Amused
- Ready to start playing

The choice feels **legitimate and fair**, because the process was structured and shared.

---

### Tone Characteristics

The voice of the app should feel:

- Mystical
- Ceremonial
- Playfully dramatic
- Calmly confident

It should never feel:

- Snarky
- Mocking
- Random
- Chaotic
- Overly comedic

The tone should suggest **ancient board-game fate magic**, but delivered lightly and clearly.

---

### Ritual Moments

The mystical tone should appear **only at meaningful moments**:

**Before filtering**

“Let us consult the table.”

**Before spinning**

“Ready to consult the fates?”

**Selection reveal**

“The wheel has spoken.”

**After reveal**

“Tonight, you shall play…”

These moments should feel like **tiny ceremonies**, not jokes.

---

### Personality Boundaries

The ritual framing must always sit on top of a **clear and accessible interface**.

Plain language remains the default for instructions and navigation.

Mystical language appears only during moments of anticipation or resolution.

This ensures the app stays:

- readable
- predictable
- neurodivergent-friendly

while still delivering a memorable personality.

---

### Brand Essence

**A playful ritual that turns indecision into destiny.**

# Current App Structure (v1)

## Navigation Structure

Single stack navigator.

**Primary screens (conceptually):**

- Splash
- Welcome
- ConnectBGG
- CollectionImported
- Home
- MyGames
- Setup
- Results
- SelectedGame

Spinner is presented as a modal overlay during selection (implementation detail may vary), but the **product flow** is: narrow → vote/weight → spin/select → celebrate.

---

## First-Time User Flow

1. **Splash**
    - Title: “What Should We Play?”
    - Subtitle: “A Board Game Decision Tool”
    - Attribution: “Data provided by BoardGameGeek”
    - Auto-routing based on whether collection data exists locally
2. **Welcome** (shown when no collection exists)
    - Copy: "Connect your BoardGameGeek collection to find the perfect game for your group."
    - CTA: "Connect BoardGameGeek"
3. **ConnectBGG**
    - Username entry
    - Loads collection from BGG API
    - Caches locally on-device
    - On success, navigates to CollectionImported
4. **CollectionImported**
    - Success confirmation: "X games added to your collection."
    - CTA: "Start Picking" → Home
5. **Home**
    - Primary actions:
        - Choose A Game
        - Browse Collection
        - Settings (includes Change Username)

---

## Core Decision Flow

1. **Home**
    - Primary CTA: **Choose A Game**
2. **Setup**
    - Load a Preset (optional)
    - Player count (required)
    - Optional filters:
        - Play Time (max length)
        - Complexity (“How complex are you willing to go?”)
        - Advanced: Mechanics, Categories (collapsible)
    - Save/overwrite presets inline
3. **Results**
    - Displays eligible games
    - Supports weighted input for group fairness
4. **Spinner / Selection Moment**
    - Selection resolves from the weighted pool
    - The outcome is revealed with ceremony
5. **SelectedGame**
    - Confetti celebration
    - Clear outcome
    - Actions:
        - Start New Pick
        - Pick Again (Same Filters)

---

## Quick Spin vs Vote First (v1)

Both paths exist to support different group moods:

### Quick Spin

Used when:

- the group wants speed
- nobody wants to negotiate
- “just pick something” is the vibe

Behavior:

- the filtered pool is used directly
- the app picks via spinner/selection immediately

### Vote First (Weighted)

Used when:

- the group wants fairness
- people have preferences but don’t want debate
- you want “popular is more likely” without consensus pressure

Behavior:

- each player contributes input (one vote per player)
- games receive weight based on votes
- spinner resolves with the weighted distribution

This weighted ritual is a core differentiator: it preserves agency while avoiding negotiation.

- **MyGames** (header: "Browse Collection")
    - A browsable list of games (thumbnail, name, year, rating)
    - Refresh/sync option
    - "Choose a Game" CTA at bottom

- **Settings**
    - Change Username
    - TODO: Light/dark toggle (currently theme follows system)
    - (Other small preferences as needed)

## Presets (v1)

Presets are a significant feature that reduce friction for returning users and common group configurations.

- **Quick presets** (built-in): Party Night, Heavy Night, Family
- **User-saved presets**: save, load, rename, overwrite, delete (max 15)
- **Load on Setup**: "Load a Preset" opens modal with quick + saved presets
- **Save from Setup**: save current filter state (including mechanics/categories) as new preset or overwrite existing
- **Save from Results**: "Save as preset" after filtering (same constraints)

# Known Weaknesses / Suspected Friction

## Visual Design

- Overall aesthetic feels dated and flat.
- Lacks visual personality consistent with the “mischievous but fair” tone.
- Color system feels unintentional.
- UI does not feel modern or cohesive.

## Action Hierarchy

- Primary vs secondary actions are not always clearly distinguished.
- Some screens feel like “forms” instead of a guided ritual.
- The moment of selection needs stronger payoff.

## Copy & Tone

- Copy does not consistently reflect the ritual-keeper voice.
- Emotional payoff moments may not feel elevated enough yet.

## Overall Feeling

- More like a utility tool than a playful decision companion.
- Needs stronger intentionality in hierarchy and celebration moments.

# Design Direction Goals

## Theme Foundation

The app must feel **clear, calm, and intentional first**.

The ritual framing adds personality, but the interface must always remain simple and understandable.

Neurodivergent-friendly design principles apply:

- Clear visual hierarchy
- Predictable layouts
- Strong contrast
- Minimal visual noise
- No hidden rules or surprising behavior

The ritual should feel **comforting and structured**, not chaotic.

---

## Modes

The app supports both:

- **Light Mode**
- **Dark Mode**

Theme currently follows system preference. TODO: Add manual light/dark toggle in Settings.

Dark mode should feel especially comfortable in game-night lighting and slightly atmospheric — supporting the ritual tone without becoming dramatic or distracting.

---

## Visual Personality

The visual design should support the feeling of **a small ritual taking place.**

This can be expressed through:

- Subtle glow effects
- Soft motion during the spinner moment
- Confetti or celebration at the reveal
- Slightly mystical visual accents

The aesthetic should feel:

- Cozy
- Warm
- Slightly magical
- Intentionally calm

It should never feel:

- Chaotic
- Loud
- Overly decorative
- Distracting

---

## Emotional Design Vibe

Primary: **Cozy Ritual**

Secondary: **Playful Anticipation**

The experience should feel:

- Warm
- Inviting
- Ceremonial
- Lightly magical
- Decisive

The ritual builds anticipation and then resolves it quickly.

---

## Where the Magic Appears

Mystical elements should appear **only at meaningful moments**, such as:

- The spinner moment
- The final reveal
- Celebration states
- Occasional microcopy

Everyday interface elements should remain **clear and grounded**.

This keeps the product accessible while still giving the decision moment a sense of **ceremony and delight**.

# Product Pillars

## 1. Decision Ritual, Not Decision Tool

WhatShouldWePlay is not just a filter or randomizer.

It transforms the awkward moment of choosing a game into a **short ritual the group performs together**.

The flow intentionally follows a ceremonial structure:

1. Narrow the possibilities
2. Offer preferences
3. Let fate resolve the outcome

The ritual replaces negotiation and removes the burden of choosing from any one person.

If a feature does not support this ritual, it likely does not belong in the product.

---

## 2. Clarity Over Cleverness

The primary user experiences cognitive load around decision-making.

The interface must therefore be:

- predictable
- readable at a glance
- low-friction
- emotionally calming

Mystical tone and personality should **never interfere with usability**.

If personality conflicts with clarity, clarity always wins.

---

## 3. Shared Fairness

The system must feel fair to everyone at the table.

Features like weighted voting exist to ensure:

- every player has a voice
- popular choices are more likely
- the final outcome still feels surprising and fun

The result should feel like **the group decided together**, even though the ritual resolved the final choice.

---

## 4. Fast Path to Playing

Game night energy is fragile.

The entire experience should move the group from **indecision → game on the table** as quickly as possible.

This means:

- minimal setup friction
- few screens
- quick feedback
- decisive outcomes

The product should never feel like another game to play before the game.
